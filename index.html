Introduction¶

In this tutorial we will walk through the data science pipeline while exploring data from Twitch

Background¶

Twitch is currently one of the most popular gaming and variety streaming servives constantly hosting hundreds of thousands of creators and millions of viewers. But not everyone on the platform streams to thousands of viewers. Only a handful of creators are able to crack the upper echelon where they are able to create lucrative carrers.

However, to actually create a community and carrer on the platform seems arbitrary completly luck-based to most. We are here to explore data on some of the most successful Twitch streamers, in the hopes to find out how these creators grow their channels.

About the Data¶

The dataset consists of the top 1000 streamers from one year and their respective stats which include:

  • Channel Name
  • Watch & Stream Time (Minutes)
  • Peak & Average Viewers
  • Followers
  • Followers & Views Gained
  • Whether they are partnered with Twitch
  • Whether they stream 18+ content
  • The primary language spoken on the channel

Exploring the Relationships¶

We know these variables are closely related however we do not know how they correlate. We will plot them against each other to pull some potentially useful visual insights.

First we will plot the most strongly correlated relationship: followers against followers. We are using Seaborn's regplot over thier lineplot because regplot automatically calculates a linear trendline for us and we are most interested in that line. While we can use lineplot, it will attempt to draw a jaggy line between each data point which is not useful and more work to then add a linear regression line.

In [212]:
# plt.figure creates a canvas on which to place our seaborn plot

# we use seaborn's regplot which shows a linear regression line to visualize the trend
sns.regplot(x=data['Followers'], y=data['Followers gained'])
# plot.show() displays the plt.figure
plt.show()

The trendline shows an increasing trend between more followers and increased followers gained. This implies that those with more followers will increase said follower count at greater rates.

After confirming the previous relationship and we naturally turn our attention to the other strongly correlated relationships we found eariler the value of exploring these realtionships comes into question. Predicting with Machine Learning¶

We previously found many interesting relationships. Now we will use these relationships to develop a machine learning model to make accurate predictions.

Preparing Data¶

We will encode our model with partnered mature content and language as our categories. We will not use numerical factors as they are things that a common streamer cannot simply "have" such as followers and therefore not useful for our analysis.

We then transform and scale the categories with .fit_transform to prepare our training data without making it biased when scaling.

In [230]:
categories = ['Partnered', 'Mature', 'Language']

labels = sklearn.preprocessing.LabelEncoder()
for category in categories:
    data[category] = labels.fit_transform(data[category])

We are only using watch times and followers are they are deeply related to many of our other numerical factors so we will drop the others to reduce overlap and complexity. In addition, we have already confirmed the general trends of these two metrics and understand the relationship between them.

When exploring the numerical data we found large outliers, well seen in above plot of language vs watch time per follower in Thai. Robust scaler allows us to rescale our data to be resistent to these extreme outliers.

In [231]:
numerical = ['Watch time(Minutes)', 'Followers']

rs = sklearn.preprocessing.RobustScaler()
data[numerical] = rs.fit_transform(data[numerical])

Model Building¶

Now that we have prepared our data we can start building our model. Linear regression is a classic and simple design that tries to fit a straight line to our model and predicts based off the function produced. While not the most accurate, we have found very linear relationships in our data and could be accurately predicted with a linear regression model.

In [372]:
from sklearn.metrics import mean_squared_error
# assigns a linear regression model to reg
reg = LinearRegression()
# fits the model to our training data
reg.fit(X_train, y_train)
# predicts target values
reg_y_train = reg.predict(X_train)
reg_y_test = reg.predict(X_test)

# computes model performance by accuracy
acc_train = reg.score(X_train, y_train)
acc_test = reg.score(X_test, y_test)

rmse_train = np.sqrt(mean_squared_error(y_train, reg_y_train))
rmse_test = np.sqrt(mean_squared_error(y_test, reg_y_test))

# prints accuracy scores
print("Accuracy on training Data: {:.3f}".format(acc_train))
print("Accuracy on test Data: {:.3f}".format(acc_test))
print('RMSE on the training set is:', rmse_train)
print('RMSE on the testing set is:', rmse_test)

Conclusion¶

During this tutorial we took Twitch statistics through the data science pipeline: importing, analyzing, hypothesizing and making predictions on the data. Through this process we were able to understand how many of the numerical metrics were so closely related they can be considered the same varible at times.
